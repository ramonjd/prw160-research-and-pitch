1988 in Australia Life and events

Were I to draw solely upon my memories of my childhood in Australia during the 1980s as source material for my novel, I’d probably end up creating a golden-syrup dripping fantasy land in which birds flocked to my shoulders wherever I went and the men and women wearing oversized grey suits and shoulder pads running the country could be taken at their word.

One knows better with age, and a genuine reason why I wanted to set a story in this period, was to examine many of the events of the time, from which my pre-pubescent youth stole their relevance and interestingness.

Australia had just crawled its way out of recession and was sighing and moaning its way into another. In 1988 we were told to celebrate the 200th anniversary of English settlement, without really being told why we should, and when people dared to raise such blasphemies as the maltreatment of the Indigenous population or the recent repudiation of the white australia immigration policy conceived at the nation’s federation, their cries were drowned out by dentally endowed media commentators among the gratuitous flag-waving and shimmering barbeque haze.

Similar to my own adolescentexperience, the protagonist in my story will observe most of the events happening beyond his immediate sphere with passivity; oblivious that certain facets of the era affect him and those around him.

My research so far has primarily consisted of looking at major events of 1988, aspects of the environment in which the story is set, and also everyday life, such as the cost of living. While there's no scope to include all my notes and findings, I've provided the main themes.

Events

A selection of events that took place in 1988, which I plan to indirectly reference in my writing.

January

  • Monday, 18th

    A revamped A Current Affair debuts on Channel Nine, hosted by Jana Wendt.

    Legistimising the harrassment of overweight, foreign rip off merchants and exploiting consumers who have alleged to have landed foul of the Trading Act.

  • Sunday, 17th

    Channel 7 broadcasted the first episode of Home and Away

    Many rural towns still receive only two television channels, and that's if they're lucky. Viewers therefore greedily gobble up this white bread drama set in the fictional and chloroformic Summer Bay.

  • Tuesday, 26th

    First Fleet re-enactment on Sydney Harbour, Australia Day

    Australia marks 200 since the arrival of the 'First Fleet': eleven boats filled mostly with the convicted criminals who were to set the genetic foundation stone for many of its twenty-first century politicians.

    To celebrate, they decided to reenact the voyage (this time without the smallpox and three per cent death rates). Eleven tall ships set out from Portsmouth, England May 13, 1987 and arrived in Sydney Harbour on Jan 26.

March

  • Friday, 4th

    Footy

    The newly built Sydney Football Stadium hosts its first game of rugby league exciting fans and public drinking houses to frothingly new heights.

April

  • Saturday, 30th

    World Expo opens in Brisbane

    Brisbane finally finds itself on the international and domestic map by presenting it as sophisticated city with an global outlook, instead of an introspective backwater full of piss-swilling bogans.

May

  • Monday, 9th

    Parliament House opened

    The Queen attends the opening of the new Parliament House in Canberra. The building's architect, Romaldo Giurgola, said of his creation:

    We felt if Australia’s new Parliament House was to speak honestly about its purpose, it could not be built on top of the hill as this would symbolise government imposed upon the people.

October

  • Monday, 12th

    Walsh Street police shootings

    Two young Victorian police officers were shot down gang-style in a suburban street, leading to a spate of retaliation murders.

Where in the world?

My novel is based on the north east coastline - a temperate, subtropical environment, with lush vegetation, harsh summers, bright skies and, back then, relatively isolated rural towns. The Pacific dwarfs the land and dominates the weather patterns and much of the social life.

The Pacific, weather and the natural environment

Weather

In 1988 the average temperature in northern NSW was 23.7°C, with a high of 43.3°C in January and low of 12.3°C in June. The main summer months of 1988, January and December, received 284.2mm and 247.6mm of rain respectively. On a typical summer's day, the sun would have risen just before 6:00am and set around 8:45pm, allowing plenty of outdoor time.

Flora and fauna

Coastal plant life is diverse but there are some species which dominate the pelagic zones, including bitoe bush, casaurina pines, crab apple* trees, lantana bush and eucalypts of course.

On an average beach fishing trip, you might have caught a snapper, flathead, bream or yellow tail. The rockpools teem with life - crabs, anenomoe, sponge, snails, and weed.

Popular cars

Long before the advent of Priuses and shining 4WD's, the 'Datto 120', even with a rusted out roof, was the coolest contraption on tar, and the apogee of Australian social status was the Holden Commodore.

In 1988, petrol was cheap and the distances were great, and if you'd never cruised at 120km/ph down the highway with your elbow hanging out catching a tan, or driven back from the beach sweating like a dog in a box, you'd never lived. Or didn't have a licence.

Ford Falcon versus Holden Commodore was like Windows verse Mac today: there was just no convincing each side of the others' merits, or that they were, in reality, two versions of the same inanity.

Crime and policing

Bikie gangs

One of my lead characters is a member of a bikie group. Bikie groups have a long history of notoriety in Australia, with several groups allegedly involved in organised crime including sales of drugs, weapons and extortion.

What interested me particularly was the code of loyalty that some groups follow. Like some other cults, apostasy is not tolerated and sometimes harshly punished.

Australia Day Bicentennial celebrations and our convict heritage

The 200th anniversary of European settlement. In 1788 Captain Arthur Phillip led the eleven-strong fleet into Sydney Cove to establish the first colony. Aboard the ships were 1480 people including, officers, crew and convicts. While most of the prisoners were British, there were also Americans, French and Africans aboard.

Many of the convicts had been sentenced for crimes such as theft, violent crime, break and enter. Jane McManus, age 18, was imprisoned for the theft of goods worth two pounds, 15 shillings. Before sailing to Sydney on the convict ship, Charlotte, Jane had spent a year in a prison hulk in England.

Two hundred years later as the video below shows, Charles and Diana were looking more uncomfortable than usual together as they basted in the summer sun in front of the Opera House. Also on the day singer John Farhnam won the Australian of the Year and the biggest Indigenous land rights protest took place. It was all happening on the 26th of January, 1988. If Captain Phillip had been alive to see what he and his fleet had started two centuries earlier, he might have wondered, "How the devil did they build that peculiar bridge?"
